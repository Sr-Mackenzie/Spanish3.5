Week 3: Los aspectos del trabajo y el subjuntivo con cláusulas adjetivas

Focus: Learn the hiring process and workplace vocabulary, watch interview preparation videos, and introduce the subjunctive mood with adjective clauses to describe ideal candidates and unknown qualities.

  • Vocabulary: Los aspectos del trabajo (beneficios, carta de recomendación, contratación, curriculum vitae, entrevista, desempeño laboral, horario, mercado laboral, oferta, puesto, requisito, trabajo de medio/tiempo completo, turno) + Verbs (desempeñar, emprender, ganar dinero por su cuenta, hacer su sueño realidad, ocuparse de, solicitar un empleo)
  • Grammar: El subjuntivo con cláusulas adjetivas — ¿Conoces a alguien que...? No hay nadie que... Se busca a alguien que... Queremos emplear a gente que...
  • Culture: The hiring process, interview etiquette, recommendation letter debate, Día del trabajador
  • Modes: Interpretive Interpersonal Presentational
  • Weekly Gradebook Entry: Job Posting Creation — "Se busca..." classified ad using the subjunctive (Day 15)

11 La contratación — The Hiring Process VocabularyCulture

▼
🎯 Daily Objective (Can-Do)
I can describe the steps of the hiring process in order (solicitar, entrevista, oferta, contratación) and identify key workplace vocabulary related to getting a job.
✅ Activities & Sequence (~45 min)
  1. Warm-Up — Spiral Review (5 min): Project 3 career images. Students write a sentence for each using a function verb from Week 2: "La científica investiga nuevas bacterias." Share with a partner.
  2. Hiring Process Sequence with Slides 26–27 (12 min): Walk through the 4-step hiring process using the unit slides: asistir a una feria de empleo → solicitar empleo / enviar el CV → la entrevista → la oferta. Students create a visual flow chart in notebooks with the 4 steps, adding a key vocabulary word or phrase at each step. Introduce related verbs: solicitar un empleo, desempeñar, emprender.
  3. Vocabulary Stations (15 min): Set up 4 stations around the room, each focused on a cluster of workplace vocabulary:
    Station 1: La solicitud (CV, requisito, solicitar empleo) — students match sample CV sections to vocabulary
    Station 2: La entrevista (entrevista, puesto, oferta) — students sequence interview dialogue strips
    Station 3: El horario (horario, turno, medio tiempo, tiempo completo) — students read sample schedules and answer questions
    Station 4: El trabajo (beneficios, mercado laboral, desempeño laboral) — students match definitions
    Groups rotate every 3.5 minutes.
  4. Partner Sequence Retell (8 min): Without looking at notes, partners take turns retelling the hiring process to each other in Spanish. Listener checks accuracy using their flow chart. Switch roles. Sentence frame: "Primero, tú ______. Después, ______. Luego, ______. Finalmente, ______."
  5. Wrap-Up (5 min): Teacher quickly introduces Día del trabajador (Slide 49) as a cultural note — when is it celebrated in Latin America vs. the U.S.? Quick discussion: Why might the dates differ?
🔍 Check for Understanding
Station Accountability Sheets: Each station has a short task students complete on a recording sheet. Collect at the end — quick scan shows who understood the vocabulary at each station. Also listen during partner retell for correct sequencing and vocabulary use.
🗣 Student Discourse Strategy
Stations + Partner Retell: Stations create natural movement and small-group interaction (groups of 3–4). The partner retell afterward adds accountability — students know they need to remember what they learned at each station. Management: assign groups before class, post rotation directions clearly, use a loud timer/bell for transitions. The physical activity of moving between stations helps reset 8th-grader attention spans.
⚖ Differentiation
Heritage/Native Speakers ↑: At each station, they complete the standard task PLUS write one original sentence using the vocabulary in a real-world context. During the partner retell, they must add details beyond the 4 basic steps: "Después de solicitar empleo, es importante dar seguimiento..." They can also share personal/family anecdotes about the hiring process.
Additional Support ↓: The flow chart template is partially filled in (Step 1 completed as a model). Station recording sheets include word banks and visual cues. For the partner retell, allow students to reference their flow chart and provide the sequence words (primero, después, luego, finalmente) on a card.

12 La entrevista — Interview Prep VocabularyCulture

▼
🎯 Daily Objective (Can-Do)
I can ask and answer common job interview questions in Spanish and identify qualities of a strong candidate.
✅ Activities & Sequence (~45 min)
  1. Warm-Up — Hiring Process Recap (5 min): Students number the hiring steps 1–4 from a scrambled list on the board. Quick partner check, then whole-class confirm. Today we zoom into step 3: la entrevista.
  2. Video: Cómo prepararse para una entrevista (10 min): Play the video from Slide 34 (or an authentic YouTube clip about interview preparation in Spanish). Pre-viewing: give students 3 tips to listen for. Post-viewing: partners compare the 3 tips they heard. Whole-class share to build a master list on the board.
  3. Interview Q&A with Slides 35–36 (10 min): Present the 6 sample interview questions and model responses from the unit slides. Students copy the 6 questions into their notebooks. For each question, discuss: What is the interviewer really asking? What makes a strong answer? Introduce "cualidades de un buen candidato" (Slide 31).
  4. Mock Interview Roleplay (15 min): In pairs, students roleplay an interview. Student A is the interviewer (picks 3 of the 6 questions from their notebook). Student B is the candidate and must answer in complete sentences. After 5 minutes, switch roles. Circulate and note strong answers to highlight afterward. Post sentence frames for responses: "Solicité este trabajo porque ______." / "Poseo destrezas de ______."
  5. Recommendation Letter Debate (5 min): Quick poll using Slide 33: When should you present a carta de recomendación — before, during, or after the interview? Students raise hands, teacher tallies. Ask 2–3 students to defend their position in Spanish. No right answer — the goal is discourse.
🔍 Check for Understanding
Roleplay Observation: During mock interviews, circulate with a checklist. Note: (1) Are students using the interview questions correctly? (2) Are responses in complete sentences? (3) Are they using workplace vocabulary naturally? Flag students who struggle to form responses for additional support on Day 13.
🗣 Student Discourse Strategy
Mock Interview with Role Cards: Each student gets a role card. The interviewer card lists 3 questions they must ask (chosen from the 6). The candidate card includes sentence starters for responses. This scaffolding prevents 8th graders from going off-script into English. The role switch ensures every student practices BOTH asking and answering. Tip: pair stronger speakers with students who need support so the stronger student models first as the candidate.
⚖ Differentiation
Heritage/Native Speakers ↑: Their interviewer role card includes 2 additional improvised follow-up questions they must create on the spot. As candidates, they answer without sentence frames and add a "closing statement" at the end: "Gracias por la oportunidad. Estoy seguro/a de que puedo ______." During the debate, they present a full argument (3+ sentences) rather than just a position.
Additional Support ↓: Candidate role cards include full model answers that students can customize (swap in their own details). Limit to 2 interview questions per round instead of 3. Provide a visual reference sheet with "cualidades" vocabulary paired with images. For the video, give a simplified viewing guide with multiple-choice answers.

13 El subjuntivo con cláusulas adjetivas — Introduction Grammar

▼
🎯 Daily Objective (Can-Do)
I can understand when and why we use the subjunctive with adjective clauses and form basic sentences describing unknown or nonexistent people using "Se busca a alguien que..." and "No hay nadie que..."
✅ Activities & Sequence (~45 min)
  1. Warm-Up — Noticing Activity (7 min): Display 3 pairs of sentences on the board (from Slide 54):
    "Conozco a un muchacho que juega fútbol." vs. "¿Conoces a alguien que juegue balonmano?"
    "Tengo un amigo que trabaja como cajero." vs. "Busco a alguien que trabaje como salvavidas."
    Students discuss with a partner: What's different between the two sentences in each pair? Why does the verb change? Collect student hypotheses — don't correct yet.
  2. Grammar Explanation with Slides 51–54 (12 min): Reveal the rule: when we describe someone/something we KNOW exists → indicative. When we describe someone/something UNKNOWN, HYPOTHETICAL, or NONEXISTENT → subjunctive. Walk through the 4 trigger phrases from the vocabulary list: ¿Conoces a alguien que...? / No hay nadie que... / Queremos emplear a gente que... / Se busca a alguien que... Students copy the rule and trigger phrases with examples.
  3. Guided Practice — Indicative or Subjunctive? (10 min): Display 8 sentences. Students work in pairs to decide: indicative or subjunctive? Then write the correct verb form. Examples: "Conozco a una ingeniera que [saber/sepa] programar." vs. "Necesitamos a alguien que [saber/sepa] programar." Review together, discussing WHY for each.
  4. Job Posting Reading (10 min): Show a sample job posting (authentic or teacher-created) that uses subjunctive naturally: "Se busca a un empleado que tenga experiencia previa y que sea responsable." Students identify all subjunctive verbs in the posting and explain why each one is subjunctive (the person doesn't exist yet — they're describing an ideal candidate).
  5. Exit Ticket (6 min): Students write 2 original sentences: one using indicative (someone they know) and one using subjunctive (someone they're looking for). Frame: "Conozco a ______ que ______." / "Busco a alguien que ______."
🔍 Check for Understanding
Exit Ticket Analysis: The 2-sentence exit ticket is your key data point. Sort into 3 piles: (1) Gets both right — ready to move on. (2) Gets indicative right but struggles with subjunctive — needs more practice with forms. (3) Can't distinguish when to use each — needs the concept re-taught. Use this data to form flexible groups for Day 14.
🗣 Student Discourse Strategy
Noticing Activity → Hypothesis Building: Starting with student-generated hypotheses (rather than a teacher lecture) creates investment in the grammar concept. Partners discuss first, then share with the class. When a student gets close to the rule, celebrate it: "Almost! Let's refine that idea together." This makes grammar feel like a puzzle to solve rather than a rule to memorize. For 8th graders, the "detective" framing works well.
⚖ Differentiation
Heritage/Native Speakers ↑: Many heritage speakers use the subjunctive intuitively. Their task: during the noticing activity, they explain to their partner WHY the verb changes (they can often articulate it naturally). For the job posting, they write 3 additional subjunctive requirements for the job. Exit ticket: write 3 sentences instead of 2, and one must use "No hay nadie que..."
Additional Support ↓: Provide a color-coded reference card: GREEN = indicative (known, real), RED = subjunctive (unknown, looking for). The guided practice includes the first 3 answers completed as models. For the exit ticket, provide sentence starters with the trigger phrase already written: "Busco a alguien que [sepa/sea/tenga] ______."

14 Subjuntivo adjetival — Applied Practice GrammarVocabulary

▼
🎯 Daily Objective (Can-Do)
I can use the subjunctive with adjective clauses to describe ideal job candidates and discuss what employers look for in employees, integrating workplace vocabulary.
✅ Activities & Sequence (~45 min)
  1. Warm-Up — Subjunctive Conjugation Drill (5 min): Rapid-fire: teacher gives an infinitive, students write the subjunctive "yo" form on whiteboards. Do 8 verbs mixing regular and stem-changers: trabajar→trabaje, tener→tenga, ser→sea, saber→sepa, poder→pueda, etc. Immediate visual feedback.
  2. Flexible Groups — Based on Exit Ticket Data (15 min): Use yesterday's exit ticket to form 3 groups:
    Group A (re-teach): Teacher-led small group. Reteach indicative vs. subjunctive concept using physical manipulatives: green cards = real/known, red cards = unknown/searching. Practice 6 sentences together.
    Group B (practice): Independent worksheet with 10 fill-in-the-blank sentences choosing between indicative and subjunctive forms. Self-check with answer key when done.
    Group C (extend): Read an authentic job posting from a Spanish-language job site. Identify all subjunctive uses, then write their OWN job posting for a dream business (preview of tomorrow's assessment).
  3. Whole Class — "El empleador ideal" Discussion (12 min): Rejoin as a whole class. Teacher poses: "Ustedes son los dueños de un restaurante nuevo. ¿Qué tipo de empleado buscan?" Students brainstorm using subjunctive: "Buscamos a alguien que sea puntual." "Necesitamos un camarero que atienda bien a los clientes." "No queremos a nadie que sea perezoso." Write responses on the board, building a collaborative "ideal employee" description.
  4. Mafalda Comic Analysis (8 min): Display a Mafalda comic strip related to work (Slide 50). Students read and discuss in pairs: What is the humor? What comment is Mafalda making about work/life? Does this connect to any vocabulary we've learned? Brief whole-class share.
  5. Preview Assessment (5 min): Explain tomorrow's gradebook task: creating a "Se busca..." job posting. Show the model. Students begin brainstorming which job they want to create a posting for.
🔍 Check for Understanding
"El empleador ideal" Whiteboard Contributions: During the whole-class brainstorm, have each student write ONE sentence on a small whiteboard before sharing. This ensures everyone produces a subjunctive sentence (not just the volunteers). Scan the room for accuracy before they share aloud. Also check Group B worksheets — target 80% accuracy to confirm readiness for tomorrow's assessment.
🗣 Student Discourse Strategy
"El empleador ideal" Collaborative Brainstorm: This is a discourse-rich whole-class activity where every contribution uses the target grammar. Key management: require whiteboards FIRST (individual accountability), then volunteers share. Build the list on the board so students see the subjunctive pattern repeated 10+ times. For 8th graders, make it fun: "Your restaurant is about to open. You need the PERFECT team. What do you demand?" The ownership element drives engagement.
⚖ Differentiation
Heritage/Native Speakers ↑: In the flexible group time, they work on the authentic job posting extension (Group C). During the whole-class brainstorm, they must contribute sentences using more complex structures: "Queremos emplear a gente que no solo sea puntual sino que también demuestre liderazgo." For the Mafalda comic, they write a 3-sentence analysis explaining the cultural commentary.
Additional Support ↓: In the flexible group time, they receive small-group re-teaching (Group A). During the brainstorm, they can reference the trigger phrase list from yesterday. Provide a "subjunctive verb cheat sheet" with the 10 most common verbs already conjugated. For Mafalda, provide guided questions and allow discussion in a mix of English and Spanish.

15 Weekly Assessment — "Se busca..." Job Posting Gradebook

▼
🎯 Daily Objective (Can-Do)
I can write a job posting for a position of my choice using the subjunctive with adjective clauses to describe the ideal candidate and workplace vocabulary to specify job requirements.
✅ Activities & Sequence (~45 min)
  1. Warm-Up — Model Analysis (5 min): Display the authentic job posting from the final exam reading section (the barber shop posting) or a teacher-created model. Students identify: What subjunctive phrases do you see? What vocabulary from our unit appears? This refreshes both grammar and vocabulary right before the assessment.
  2. Assessment Overview & Rubric (3 min): Display the rubric. Review requirements. Answer clarifying questions. Students may use vocabulary notes but NOT translators.
  3. Assessment — "Se busca..." Job Posting (28 min): Students create a job posting (on Canvas text entry, Google Doc, or paper) for a job/career of their choice.

    Required elements:
    • Título del puesto — Name and brief description of the job
    • Requisitos — At least 4 sentences using subjunctive with adjective clauses describing the ideal candidate ("Se busca a alguien que...", "Necesitamos una persona que...", "No queremos a nadie que...")
    • Aspectos del trabajo — Horario, turno, tipo de trabajo (medio tiempo/tiempo completo), beneficios
    • Funciones — At least 2 job functions the employee will perform
    • Cómo solicitar — Instructions for applying (send CV, attend interview, etc.)
    • Use at least 8 unit vocabulary words total (across all weeks)
  4. Peer Review — "Would You Apply?" (6 min): Students swap postings with a partner. Partner reads and answers: "¿Solicitarías este empleo? ¿Por qué sí o no?" They write a 2-sentence response on a sticky note or Canvas comment. This adds an interpersonal reading + writing element.
  5. Submit & Week 3 Wrap-Up (3 min): Submit to Canvas. Can-Do Statements self-check. Preview: "Next week we learn about job competencies, the future tense, and si clauses — what will happen IF you pursue your dream career!"
📋 Gradebook Entry — Week 3 All-Task

"Se busca..." — Job Posting with Subjunctive

Mode: Presentational Writing  |  Time: 28 min  |  Points: Rubric-based (adjusted MCPS Intermediate Learner Presentational Rubric)

Requirements: Complete job posting with 4+ subjunctive sentences, job aspects, 2+ functions, application instructions, and 8+ vocabulary words.

Task Completion: All 5 required sections present; meets vocabulary minimum (8 words across all unit weeks)
Subjunctive Accuracy: 4+ correct subjunctive sentences using adjective clause triggers; clear distinction from indicative
Vocabulary: Accurate, contextualized use of workplace, career, and function vocabulary
Comprehensibility & Format: Reads like a realistic job posting; organized and understandable to a sympathetic reader
🔍 Check for Understanding
The posting IS the CFU for subjunctive mastery. When grading, categorize errors: (1) Uses subjunctive triggers but wrong verb form — needs conjugation practice. (2) Uses correct forms but wrong trigger context — needs concept reinforcement. (3) Avoids subjunctive entirely — needs significant re-teaching. Plan Week 4 warm-ups to address the most common error category. Also check: Are students integrating vocabulary from ALL 3 weeks, or only the most recent?
🗣 Student Discourse Strategy
"Would You Apply?" Peer Review: This 6-minute activity turns a solo writing assessment into a discourse opportunity. The question "¿Solicitarías?" requires students to READ a classmate's posting carefully and formulate an opinion. Post the response frame: "Sí, solicitaría porque ______" or "No solicitaría porque ______." This also serves as a comprehensibility check — if the reader can't understand the posting well enough to respond, that's valuable feedback.
⚖ Differentiation
Heritage/Native Speakers ↑: Extended requirements: 6+ subjunctive sentences (including "No hay nadie que..." and "Queremos emplear a gente que..."), 10+ vocabulary words, and a closing paragraph explaining why this company is a great place to work (using indicative to contrast with the subjunctive requirements section). Peer review must include a detailed response of 4+ sentences with a counter-offer: "Yo solicitaría, pero pediría ______."
Additional Support ↓: Provide a posting template with section headers and sentence starters pre-written: "Se busca a alguien que [sea/tenga/sepa] ______." Reduce to 3 subjunctive sentences and 5 vocabulary words. Allow the subjunctive verb conjugation reference card during writing. For peer review, give a simplified response frame with checkboxes: "El puesto parece: interesante / difícil / aburrido porque ______."
